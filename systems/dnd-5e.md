# System flavor: Dungeons & Dragons 5e

Grounding for the campaign-building AI. Use this to ask questions that fit how D&D campaigns actually get run. (Covers the 2014 fifth edition and the compatible 2024 revision; if the GM names an edition, follow it.)

## Feel and themes
Heroic high fantasy, tonally flexible — it can run gritty, epic, mythic, or lighthearted. Stories are usually built around an adventuring party growing in power as they confront a rising threat. Good campaigns balance three pillars: combat, exploration, and social interaction.

## Core mechanic (just enough to ask good questions)
Roll a d20, add an ability modifier plus proficiency, compare to a Difficulty Class or Armor Class; advantage/disadvantage swings the odds. Characters advance from level 1 to 20 in roughly four **tiers of play**: local heroes (1–4), heroes of the realm (5–10), masters of the realm (11–16), masters of the world (17–20). Where a campaign starts and which tiers it's meant to reach shapes everything about its scope.

## Characters
Players pick a class and subclass, a species/lineage, and a background. The party composition (who's the frontline, who casts, who talks, who scouts) matters for what challenges land. Worth surfacing in questions: the intended party size, starting level, and whether anyone has character hooks the world should answer to.

## Magic and its limits
Spellcasters use spell slots across spell levels 1–9; many key effects require concentration (only one at a time), verbal/somatic/material components, and slots that don't recover until a rest. That scarcity is the brake on magic. Magic items are GM-granted rewards, not bought freely (unless the GM says otherwise). How common and how high-magic the world is, is a real worldbuilding decision.

## Campaign structure the system rewards
A central villain (the "big bad") with a concrete plan and a sense of escalating clock; lieutenants and factions beneath them; a strong inciting hook; and a session-one situation (often a contained dungeon, heist, or crisis) that grabs the table fast. Campaigns range from tight published-style adventure paths to open sandboxes. Downtime, bases, and recurring NPCs deepen long games.

## Good things to ask about as the campaign builds
Starting level and target arc; setting (homebrew vs. a published world like the Forgotten Realms or Eberron); the villain's goal and timeline; the major factions and power players; the opening hook and first dungeon/encounter; how each PC is tied to the stakes; magic prevalence; and the intended length and pacing of the campaign.
