# System flavor: Avatar Legends — The Roleplaying Game (Magpie Games)

Grounding for the campaign-building AI. Use this to ask questions that fit the world of Avatar: The Last Airbender / The Legend of Korra and how this Powered-by-the-Apocalypse system runs.

## Feel and themes
Stories of bravery, friendship, and doing what's right against long odds, set across the Four Nations (Water, Earth, Fire, Air) and the United Republic. The beating heart is personal growth and **balance** — inner conflict and change matter as much as any external villain. Combat is about pressure, relationships, and growth, not lethality.

## Core mechanic (just enough to ask good questions)
Powered by the Apocalypse: roll **2d6 + a stat** (Creativity, Focus, Harmony, Passion). 10+ is a clean success, 7–9 succeeds with a cost or complication, and a 6 or lower is a miss that hands the GM a move. There are no hit points and **no player death**. Instead:
- **Fatigue** — a five-box track you spend to fuel techniques and mark when struck.
- **Conditions** — emotional states (afraid, angry, guilty, insecure, troubled) that penalize relevant moves; five marked conditions take a character out of the scene.
- **Balance** — a seven-point track stretched between two opposing **principles** unique to each character. It shifts through play and is the core of their arc.

## Characters
Characters are built from **playbooks**, each defining a role and two conflicting balance principles (for example, Restraint vs. Results, or Force vs. Care). A character has a **training** (which bending — water, earth, fire, air — or a non-bending Technique/weapon style) and a fighting **approach** (techniques sort into Advance & Attack, Defend & Respond, and Evade & Observe). The party usually forms a tight-knit **group** with a shared backstory.

## "Magic" and its limits
Bending isn't a spell list — it's resolved through moves and techniques, most of which cost **fatigue** to use, so power is paced by exhaustion and emotional state rather than slots. The fiction (training, mastery, the elements, the spirit world) sets what's possible. Bending mastery, spirits, and legendary techniques are earned through story and growth.

## Setting and era
The GM and table choose an **era**, each with its own conflicts and themes: **Kyoshi** (a hard, lawless age), **Roku** (an uneasy peace before war), the **Hundred Year War** (resistance against conquest), **Aang** (rebuilding and reconciliation), and **Korra** (modernization, redemption, tradition vs. technology). The era's Avatar usually sits on the fringe of the story as a powerful figure, not a party member.

## Campaign structure the system rewards
Choose the era and the group's shared bond; build a central conflict and an antagonist group whose pressure forces the PCs to confront their balance principles; and design situations, NPCs, and dilemmas that test each character's two principles rather than just threatening their bodies. NPCs come in tiers (minor, major, master) and the spirit world is always available as a source of wonder and trouble.

## Good things to ask about as the campaign builds
Which era and why; the group's origin and shared training; the nations and locations the story moves through; the central conflict and the antagonist group driving it; the specific balance-principle dilemmas you want to put in front of each PC; key NPCs and any spirits; and the kinds of growth and relationship moments you most want the campaign to create.
